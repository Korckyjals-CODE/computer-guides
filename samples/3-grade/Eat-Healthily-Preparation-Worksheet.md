# Eat Healthily: Data, Tables, and Charts

**Grade 3 · Worksheet · About 25 minutes**  
*Work with pen and paper. Use color pencils if you like. You do not need a computer or the internet.*

---

## How to use this worksheet

- Do the parts **in order** from top to bottom.
- Each part has a **time** so you know how long to spend.
- Read every instruction before you write. You can do this on your own.

---

## Part 1 – What you will learn (about 3 minutes)

**What you will learn:** In this worksheet you will learn about **data** (information we collect), **tables** (how we organize it), **calculations** (adding and working with numbers), and **charts** (pictures that show data). These ideas help us understand healthy eating and can be used with spreadsheets or apps too.

## Part 2 – Data: what can we collect? (about 5 minutes)

**Data** is information we gather—like numbers or names. When we study healthy eating, we might ask: *How many fruits did you eat today?* or *What is your favourite vegetable?*

**Do this:**

1. **Circle 2 things** we could ask people for the app (you can circle 2 of these):
   - Favourite fruit  
   - How many glasses of water they drink per day  
   - Their secret password  
   - How many vegetables they eat this week  

2. **Write one more question** you could ask someone about eating habits.

   _________________________________________

## Part 3 – Organizing data in a table (about 6 minutes)

A **spreadsheet** is like a big grid. It has **rows** (across) and **columns** (up and down). Each small box is called a **cell**. We put one piece of information in each cell. That way it is easy to read and compare.

**Do this:**

1. **Fill in this simple table.** Write the number of servings you think you should eat or drink each day (you can use 1, 2, 3…).

   | Food or drink      | Servings per day (your idea) |
   |--------------------|------------------------------|
   | Fruits             | _____                        |
   | Vegetables         | _____                        |
   | Glasses of water   | _____                        |

2. **In one short sentence**, why is it useful to put this information in a table?

   _________________________________________

## Part 4 – Calculations: what can numbers tell us? (about 5 minutes)

We can **add** and do other maths with the numbers in a table. For example: *How many fruits did someone eat in one week?* A spreadsheet (like Excel) can do the calculation for you, but you can also add with a pencil!

**Do this:**

1. **Solve this:** Monday you ate **2** fruits, Tuesday you ate **3** fruits, Wednesday you ate **2** fruits. How many fruits in total?

   Total: _____   (you can write: 2 + 3 + 2 = _____)

2. **In one short sentence**, why is it useful that the computer does the adding for you?

   _________________________________________

## Part 5 – Charts: showing data with pictures (about 4 minutes)

**Charts** (like bar charts or pie charts) let people **see** numbers quickly. We can make charts from data to show things like how many fruits someone ate—so people can understand at a glance.

**Do this:**

1. **Here are 3 numbers:** Apples **4**, Bananas **2**, Oranges **3**.  
   In the box below, draw **3 simple bars** (one for each fruit). Make the **length** of each bar match the number (4 = longest bar, 2 = shortest bar). You can use color pencils to colour each bar.

   ```
   +------------------------------------------+
   |                                          |
   |   (Draw 3 bars: Apples longest,          |
   |    Bananas shortest, Oranges in between) |
   |                                          |
   +------------------------------------------+
   ```
