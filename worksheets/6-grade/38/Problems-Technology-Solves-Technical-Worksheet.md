## Part 3 – From problem to tool (about 5 minutes)

Good designers start with the **problem**, not the gadget. They ask: *What is wrong? Who needs help? What would “better” look like?*

**Real-life scenario:** A river town floods often. Engineers place **sensors** in the water and connect them to **alert systems**. When the water rises fast, phones and sirens warn people to move to higher ground. The **problem** is dangerous, sudden flooding. The **technology** helps people get warning in time.

**Discuss with a partner or reflect in writing:** What problem is being solved? What might people do without this tool?

## Part 4 – How solutions work (about 5 minutes)

Many systems follow **input → process → output**. **Input** is what goes in (a button press, a sensor reading). **Process** is the rules or steps inside (software, a chip, gears). **Output** is what comes out (a sound, a message, movement). Some systems use **feedback**: the output is checked and the process adjusts (like a thermostat turning heat on or off).

**Real-life scenario:** A package has a **barcode**. At each stop, a worker **scans** it (input). The shipping computer **updates** the route (process). Your tracking page **shows** “Out for delivery” (output).

**Critical thinking:** Why might the same tool solve one problem but fail another? Think about accuracy, power, cost, or user error.

## Part 5 – A simple solve-it process (about 5 minutes)

You can solve problems with a short process:

1. **Define the problem** — say what is wrong and for whom.
2. **List options** — include tech and non-tech ideas.
3. **Pick and test** — try one option; see if it really helps.
4. **Improve** — fix what did not work and try again.

**Real-life scenario:** Items keep getting lost in a classroom: lunch boxes, jackets, and headphones. The class defines the problem: “Things look the same and get mixed up.” They list options: labeled cubbies, a checklist, a simple numbering system, or an app to log items. They test labeled cubbies first and add a sign-out sheet for borrowed headphones.

**Discuss with a partner or reflect in writing:** When is a low-tech fix better than an app? Give one example.
