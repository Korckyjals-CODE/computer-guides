## Part 2 – Whose problem gets solved? (about 5 minutes)

Designers and leaders choose **which problems** to work on first. A tool that helps one group might not help—or might hurt—another group if their needs were not considered.

**Real-life scenario:** A school moves all announcements to a parent app only. One family does not have a smartphone. They miss a field-trip permission form and their child cannot go. The app solved “fast messages” for many families but created a new problem for others.

**Discuss with a partner or reflect in writing:** Who benefits from the app-only plan? Who is left out? What could the school do differently?

## Part 3 – Access and fairness (about 5 minutes)

The **digital divide** means not everyone has the same access to devices, reliable **Wi-Fi**, language support, or tools that work for **disabilities** (e.g. screen readers, captions). Requiring technology for something that used to work on paper can be unfair if some people cannot use it easily.

**Real-life scenario:** Two students must complete the same online permission form tonight. Maya has a laptop, quiet room, and fast Wi-Fi. Jordan shares one phone with a parent and has spotty data. Both care about school, but Jordan needs much more time for the same task.

## Part 4 – New problems and tradeoffs (about 5 minutes)

Technology can **solve** one problem and **create** another. Examples: cameras may increase safety but also make people feel watched; social apps connect friends but can distract from homework; factories automate dull work but may change jobs for workers.

**Real-life scenario:** A school installs more security cameras after items were stolen from lockers. Some students feel safer; others feel they have no private place to relax between classes.

**Discuss with a partner or reflect in writing:** What problem do the cameras solve? What new worry might they cause for some students?

## Part 5 – Choosing wisely and speaking up (about 5 minutes)

Before adopting a “solution,” ask:

- **Do we need tech for this?**
- **Is there a simpler fix?**
- **Did we ask the people affected?**

**Real-life scenario:** A class group chat replaces study-hall check-in. The teacher wants quick answers, but some students get messages at 10 p.m. and feel they must reply to stay in good standing.

**Discuss with a partner or reflect in writing:** How could the class redesign check-in with kindness and clear boundaries?
