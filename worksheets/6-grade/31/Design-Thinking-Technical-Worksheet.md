## How to use this worksheet

- Do the parts **in order** from top to bottom.
- Each part has a **time** so you know how long to spend.

**What you will learn:** **Design thinking** is a process to solve problems creatively. ## Part 2 – The five phases of design thinking (about 5 minutes)

Design thinking has five phases. **Empathize**—understand who you help and what they need. **Define**—state the problem clearly. **Ideate**—brainstorm many ideas. **Prototype**—make a simple version. **Test**—try it and get feedback.

## Part 3 – Empathize and Define (about 5 minutes)

Before solving, you must **understand** the person you design for. What do they do? What bothers them? Then you **define** the problem in one clear sentence.

**Real-life scenario:** A team designs a lunch bag for students. They observe: students forget it, it gets squished in the backpack, and they cannot tell what is inside. They define: "Students need a lunch bag that is easy to remember, protects food, and shows what is inside."

**Discuss with a partner or reflect in writing:** Why is understanding people before solving important? What if the team had skipped this step?

**Do this:** Choose a user (e.g. a friend, family member, or yourself). Write one sentence that defines a problem they have.

## Part 4 – Ideate and Prototype (about 5 minutes)

**Ideate** means brainstorming many ideas without judging. At first, every idea counts. Then you pick the best ones and make a **prototype**—a simple sketch or model.

**Scenario:** You brainstorm 10 ways to keep a water bottle cold at school: insulated sleeve, frozen gel pack, double wall, put it in the fridge between classes, and more. You pick the best 2 ideas and sketch them.

**Critical thinking:** Why do we brainstorm many ideas before choosing one?

## Part 5 – Test and improve (about 5 minutes)

When you **test**, you show your prototype to the user. You ask what works and what does not. Then you improve it. Testing helps you find problems before you finish.

**Scenario:** A student designs a pencil holder for their desk. They show it to a friend. The friend says it tips over when they reach for a pencil. The student adds a heavier base. Now it works better.
