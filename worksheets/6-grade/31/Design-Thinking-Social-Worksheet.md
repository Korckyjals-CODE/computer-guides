# Design Thinking: Social Worksheet

**Grade 6 · Worksheet · About 25 minutes**  
*Work with pen and paper. Use color pencils if you like. You do not need a computer or the internet.*

---

## How to use this worksheet

- Do the parts **in order** from top to bottom.
- Each part has a **time** so you know how long to spend.
- Read every instruction before you write. You can do this on your own.

## Part 2 – Understanding who you design for (about 5 minutes)

The **user** is the person who will use what you design. To design well, you need to understand their life, their challenges, and what they like. Talking to the user helps you see the problem from their point of view.

**Real-life scenario:** You design a backpack for a classmate who walks 20 minutes to school and carries heavy books. You ask them: "What bothers you about your backpack?" They say it hurts their shoulders and the straps slip. Now you know you need wide straps and maybe extra padding.

**Discuss with a partner or reflect in writing:** How does talking to the user change your design? What if you had guessed instead of asking?

## Part 3 – Designing for different people (about 5 minutes)

Different users have different needs. A left-handed person may need scissors that fit their hand. A younger child may need bigger buttons. A ramp helps people in wheelchairs, while stairs work for others. One design cannot always work for everyone.

**Scenario:** A school has only stairs at the front entrance. A student who uses a wheelchair cannot get in that way. The school adds a ramp. Now everyone can enter. The ramp was designed for people who need it, but it helps others too (e.g. someone pushing a cart).

**Critical thinking:** Why can one design not work for everyone? How does thinking about different people make design better?

## Part 4 – Working in a design team (about 5 minutes)

When we design with others, we combine ideas. Listening to teammates and building on their ideas leads to better solutions. A good teammate shares ideas, listens to others, and respects different opinions.

**Scenario:** Three students design a mural for the classroom. One suggests a space theme. Another adds: "We could include our school mascot floating in space." The third says: "And each student could draw a small star with their name." Each idea builds on the last.

**Discuss with a partner or reflect in writing:** How does teamwork improve design? What happens when only one person makes all the decisions?

## Part 5 – Design that helps the community (about 5 minutes)

Design can help many people—your school, your neighborhood, or the environment. When we think about the community, we design solutions that make life better for more people.

**Scenario:** A school wants to recycle more. Students design a system: color-coded bins (blue for paper, green for plastic), labels with pictures so everyone knows where to put each item, and roles for who empties the bins each week. The whole school benefits.

**Critical thinking:** How can design make the community better? What problem in your school or neighborhood could design help solve?
