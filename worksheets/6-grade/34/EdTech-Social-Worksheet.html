How to use this worksheet
  • Do the parts in order from top to bottom.
  • Each part has a time so you know how long to spend.
  • Read every instruction before you write. You can do this on your own.

Part 1 – What you will learn (about 3 minutes)

What you will learn: School technology affects people, not just screens. In this worksheet you will think about fair access (who can use devices and when), respect and privacy around classmates’ work, honesty when you turn in digital assignments, and healthy habits so learning technology helps you without hurting focus or well-being.

Do this:

  1. Do you share devices or Wi-Fi with others at home? Circle: Often · Sometimes · Rarely
  2. Write one way digital schoolwork could feel unfair if people have different resources at home.

Part 2 – Fair access and patience (about 5 minutes)

Not everyone has the same device, quiet space, or internet at home. At school, computer carts and lab seats are often shared. Fair access means taking turns, following time limits, and not judging people for what they do or do not have.

Real-life scenario: Two students need to finish the same online practice before lunch. There is one free computer. They agree: each gets 10 minutes, then they switch. They both finish on time.

Discuss with a partner or reflect in writing: Why are agreements and turns important when technology is limited? What could go wrong if one person hogs the device?

Do this: List two fair rules your class could use when sharing devices or stations.

Part 3 – Respect and privacy on screens (about 5 minutes)

A classmate’s screen may show grades, messages, or personal information. Looking without permission is not respectful. Ask before you look or touch someone else’s device. In comments or chat, use a calm tone and the same kindness you would use face to face.

Real-life scenario: During independent work, Jordan leans over to “help” and reads Riley’s answers without asking. Riley feels exposed and upset. The teacher reminds everyone: ask first, and keep your eyes on your own work unless you are invited to collaborate.

Critical thinking: How is “peeking” at a screen similar to or different from reading someone’s paper on a desk without permission?

Do this: Write one respectful sentence you could post in a class discussion when you disagree with someone’s idea.

Part 4 – Honesty and collaboration (about 5 minutes)

Your own work means ideas and answers you understand and can explain. Sometimes teachers allow group submissions or partners—that is collaboration when the rules say so. Copying answers you do not understand, or logging in as someone else, breaks trust and academic honesty.

Real-life scenario: A friend messages: “Send me a photo of your worksheet upload so I can copy it fast.” You know the teacher asked for individual work. Saying no and offering to explain a step instead protects both of you.

Discuss with a partner or reflect in writing: How can you help a friend without doing their assignment for them?

Do this: Circle the best response when you feel pressured to share your finished file. Then explain in one sentence why.

  • A) Send the file so they stay your friend
  • B) Say no and suggest they ask the teacher for extra help
  • C) Change a few words so it looks different

Part 5 – Group work, breaks, and trusted adults (about 5 minutes)

In group EdTech projects, fair teams agree on roles (who researches, who writes, who presents). One person should not do everything in the app while others watch. Short breaks (stretch, water, eyes off the screen) can help focus. If someone bullies you online, asks for your password, or shares something that scares you, tell a trusted adult at school or home.

Real-life scenario: For a slide deck, one student creates every slide while teammates chat. Before the next class, the group agrees on a checklist: each person owns one section and initials the notes in the shared plan.

Discuss with a partner or reflect in writing: Why is it unfair if one person does almost all the digital work in a group? How does that hurt learning for everyone?
