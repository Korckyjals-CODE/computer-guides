## Part 1 – What you will learn (about 3 minutes)

**What you will learn:** **EdTech** (education technology) means using digital tools to teach and learn. Many schools use a **learning platform**—one organized place for lessons, files, and turn-in work. Some people also call that kind of system an **LMS** (learning management system). In this worksheet you will learn common parts of these systems, **input** and **output**, and why **saving** is not always the same as **submitting**.

## Part 2 – Parts of a learning platform (about 5 minutes)

## Part 3 – Input and output (about 5 minutes)

**Input** is what **you send** to the system (typing an answer, uploading a file, clicking “turn in”). **Output** is what **the system shows you** (the assignment page, a message, a score, a “submitted” notice).

**Real-life scenario:** You type your answers in a quiz. Your typing is **input**. When the screen shows “Question 3 of 10” and a timer, that is **output**. When you click **Submit**, that click is **input**. When the screen shows “You scored 8/10,” that is **output**.

**Discuss with a partner or reflect in writing:** Why is it useful to know the difference between input and output when something goes wrong (e.g. “I did the work but the teacher cannot see it”)?

## Part 4 – Saved, submitted, and due dates (about 5 minutes)

**Saving** keeps your work in your draft or file so **you** can keep editing. **Submitting** (or “turn in”) usually sends a **final copy** to the teacher so **they** can grade it. Some systems save automatically; others need you to click **Save**. If you only save but never submit, the teacher may not see your finished work.

**Real-life scenario:** Alex finishes a report on Tuesday night and clicks **Save**. The teacher asks on Wednesday why nothing was turned in. Alex realizes they never clicked **Submit**. The file was safe for Alex, but it was not delivered to the class folder.

**Critical thinking:** Why might schools separate “save draft” from “submit”? What is one risk if submit and save were always the same button?

## Part 5 – Feedback, order, and tradeoffs (about 5 minutes)

Platforms can give **instant feedback** (e.g. “correct” on a multiple-choice item) or **teacher feedback** (longer comments). Instant feedback is fast; teacher comments can explain *why* and help you grow. **Clear titles** and a sensible **order** in the class feed help everyone find the right task and due date.

**Real-life scenario:** Two classes post new work the same day. One teacher uses titles like “Week 5 – Science Lab (due Mon)” and pins the current unit at the top. Another posts five items all named “Homework.” Which class is easier to navigate? Why?

**Discuss with a partner or reflect in writing:** Can instant feedback ever teach a habit that is not helpful for harder tests later? Explain with one example.
