Part 1 – What you will learn (about 3 minutes)

What you will learn: Technology impact means the effects a tool or system has on people, places, or other systems. Some effects are intended (planned) and some are unintended (not planned). In this worksheet you will learn to notice systems (inputs → process → outputs), classify impacts, and think about trade-offs when choosing technology.

Do this:

  1. In one sentence, what is technology impact?

  2. Name one technology you use every day (e.g. phone, laptop, game console).

Part 2 – Intended and unintended impact (about 5 minutes)

Intended impact is what designers or leaders hope will happen (e.g. faster work, easier communication). Unintended impact is what happens anyway, even if no one planned it (e.g. some people are left out, new problems appear). Efficiency means doing more with less time or effort. Automation means machines or software do tasks that people used to do.

Real-life scenario: A school switches to online assignment submission. Teachers grade faster and papers are not lost. Some students do not have reliable internet at home and cannot upload work on time.

Discuss with a partner or reflect in writing: What was the intended impact? What might be unintended?

Part 3 – Data trails in digital systems (about 5 minutes)

Many apps and online services leave data trails—records of what you do (logs, clicks, time spent, location). Data is not good or bad by itself; it depends on how it is collected, stored, and used.

Real-life scenario: A fitness app tracks your steps and your location. The company wants to sell aggregated data (combined information from many users) to advertisers.

Discuss with a partner or reflect in writing: What kinds of data might this app collect? Who could benefit? Who could be harmed?

Do this: Match each data type to why it could matter. Use the word bank. Each word is used once.

Part 4 – Resource and environmental impact (about 5 minutes)

Technology also has resource impact: it uses energy, needs materials to build devices, and creates e-waste (old electronics thrown away). A longer device lifespan (using a device longer before replacing it) often means less waste.

Part 5 – Evaluating trade-offs (about 5 minutes)

Choosing technology often means trade-offs: you gain something and give up something else. More features can mean more data collection or more energy use.

Discuss with a partner or reflect in writing: Why might a “free” app still have a cost for users?

Do this: A school needs a chat app for class announcements.

  • Option A: Free app with ads; collects usage data to show targeted ads.
  • Option B: Paid app with no ads; fewer features; limited storage.
