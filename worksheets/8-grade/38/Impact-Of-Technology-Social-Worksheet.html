Impact of Technology: Social Worksheet

Grade 8 · Worksheet · About 25 minutes
Work with pen and paper. Use color pencils if you like. You do not need a computer or the internet.

How to use this worksheet
  • Do the parts in order from top to bottom.
  • Each part has a time so you know how long to spend.
  • Read every instruction before you write. You can do this on your own.

Part 1 – What you will learn (about 3 minutes)

What you will learn: Social impact means how technology changes relationships, access to opportunities, jobs, and well-being. In this worksheet you will learn about the digital divide, healthy boundaries online, ethics when sharing, and actions you can take in your community.

Do this:

  1. In one sentence, what is social impact of technology?

  2. Name one way technology changed how you talk to friends or family (e.g. group chat, video call, social feed).

Part 2 – Who benefits and who is left out (about 5 minutes)

The digital divide means unequal access to devices, reliable internet, or digital skills. When homework, jobs, or services move online, people without access can be left behind—even when the change helps others.

Real-life scenario: A town adds free Wi‑Fi downtown but not in rural neighborhoods. Teachers assign homework that must be submitted online.

Discuss with a partner or reflect in writing: Who likely gains? Who might struggle?

Do this:

Students with home internet
Students in rural areas with weak signal
Families who share one phone
Local businesses downtown

Critical thinking: Write one fair policy the town or school could try so more students can complete online work (e.g. library hours, loaner devices, offline alternatives).

Part 3 – Communication and well-being (about 5 minutes)

Technology can connect people, but it can also create pressure (comparison, fear of missing out) or hurt feelings. Boundaries are limits you set—for example, no messages after a certain time, or asking before sharing someone’s photo.

Real-life scenario: A group chat gets busy at night. One student sees memes that seem to make fun of them. They feel excluded and cannot sleep well.

Discuss with a partner or reflect in writing: How could group rules help without banning all technology?

Do this: Write 2 group norms (rules) that could help this chat. For each norm, circle what it mainly protects: privacy, kindness, or rest.

Part 4 – Ethics and responsibility (about 5 minutes)

Consent means getting permission before you share someone’s photo, video, or personal story. Privacy means respecting what others want to keep to themselves. Respect online is the same idea as respect in person: treat others the way you would want to be treated.

Part 5 – Personal and community action (about 5 minutes)

You can influence how technology is used around you. Small, steady actions often work better than trying to change everything at once.

Do this: Design a digital impact pledge—three actions you are willing to try (examples: ask before posting, balance screen time, speak up about mean comments, help a classmate with tech trouble).

Critical thinking:

  • Which pledge will be hardest for you? Why?
  • In one sentence, how could adults or your school support healthy technology use without controlling every choice?
