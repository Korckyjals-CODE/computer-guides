## Part 2 – Who benefits and who is left out (about 5 minutes)

The **digital divide** means unequal access to devices, reliable internet, or digital skills. When homework, jobs, or services move online, people without access can be left behind—even when the change helps others.

**Real-life scenario:** A town adds free Wi‑Fi downtown but not in rural neighborhoods. Teachers assign homework that must be submitted online.

**Discuss with a partner or reflect in writing:** Who likely gains? Who might struggle?

**Do this:**

| Students in rural areas with weak signal | _____ |

| Local businesses downtown | _____ |

## Part 3 – Communication and well-being (about 5 minutes)

Technology can connect people, but it can also create **pressure** (comparison, fear of missing out) or hurt feelings. **Boundaries** are limits you set—for example, no messages after a certain time, or asking before sharing someone’s photo.

**Real-life scenario:** A group chat gets busy at night. One student sees memes that seem to make fun of them. They feel excluded and cannot sleep well.

**Discuss with a partner or reflect in writing:** How could group **rules** help without banning all technology?

**Do this:** **Write 2 group norms** (rules) that could help this chat. For each norm, circle what it mainly protects: **privacy**, **kindness**, or **rest**.

## Part 4 – Ethics and responsibility (about 5 minutes)

**Consent** means getting permission before you share someone’s photo, video, or personal story. **Privacy** means respecting what others want to keep to themselves. **Respect** online is the same idea as respect in person: treat others the way you would want to be treated.

**Real-life scenario:** A friend posts a photo of you at a party without asking. The post gets many comments and shares. You feel embarrassed.
