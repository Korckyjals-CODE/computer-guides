## Part 2 – Equity: who the product “favors” (about 5 minutes)

**Equitable design** asks who might be left out if the “normal user” is assumed to have a new device, fast internet, quiet space, strong reading skills, or full mobility. Features that reward **speed**, **visibility**, or **constant logins** can accidentally punish students who work late shifts, share devices, or need more time.

**Real-life scenario:** A class app shows a **public streak** for daily logins. Students with stable homes climb the list; a student who cares for siblings after school drops off and feels labeled as “not trying.”

**Discuss with a partner or reflect in writing:** How could the same motivation goal be redesigned so **effort** is honored without shaming people who cannot log in every day?

## Part 3 – Data, nudges, and transparency (about 5 minutes)

School tools may collect **usage data** (time on screen, clicks, quiz attempts). **Nudges** are small pushes—reminders, badges, red dots—meant to change behavior. They can support habits or create **stress**. **Transparency** means students and families understand what is collected, why, and who can see it, in language they can act on.

**Scenario:** A vendor’s privacy page is 40 screens long and says the company may use “aggregated” data for “product improvement.” Your school board is deciding whether to renew the contract.

**Critical thinking:** Who gains the most if almost no one reads the privacy page? Name **two questions** you would want a student representative to ask at a school meeting before renewal.

## Part 4 – Participation and voice in product decisions (about 5 minutes)

**Stakeholders** include students, teachers, families, IT staff, and companies. Good processes invite **feedback early** (paper tests, pilot classes, surveys in home languages) instead of only fixing problems after a full launch. Ignoring **edge cases**—slow readers, new English learners, assistive tech users—often means those groups pay the hidden cost of “efficient” rollouts.

**Real-life scenario:** A district replaces a familiar gradebook with a new system over winter break. Training is one optional video. By February, teachers are exhausted and students cannot find old feedback.

## Part 5 – Ethics: guilt, pressure, and fair policies (about 5 minutes)

Some interfaces use **guilt messaging** (“You are letting your team down!”), **hidden costs** (extra steps to say “no thanks”), or **endless scroll** that makes it hard to leave. **Fair policies** set limits: quiet hours, no grades based on optional streaks, clear rules for when nudges are allowed in school, and support for opting into accessibility modes without stigma.

**Scenario:** A wellness module in a learning app sends weekend push alerts: “Don’t lose your progress!” Students report anxiety and guilt even when they finished the required work on Friday.

**Critical thinking:** Write **one sentence** that could belong in a **fair school policy** about weekend notifications from required learning apps.
