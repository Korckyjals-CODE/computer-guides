Part 1 – What you will learn (about 3 minutes)

What you will learn: Product design is the careful planning of a tool so real people can reach their goals without confusion. In this worksheet you will learn how a user goal differs from a feature, how clear layout and labels guide attention, what constraints designers must respect (accessibility, language, errors), and how tradeoffs shape a final product.

Do this:

  1. Name one learning-related app or site you use (course page, practice app, library search—any example).
  2. In one sentence, describe a moment when that tool felt confusing or harder than it needed to be.

Part 2 – User goals vs features (about 5 minutes)

A user goal is what someone is trying to accomplish (e.g. “turn in my lab before midnight with the right file type”). A feature is something the product offers (e.g. “drag-and-drop upload,” “due date countdown”). Good design starts from goals, then picks features that truly support them—not the other way around.

Real-life scenario: A student opens the course page and sees five buttons that all sound similar: “Submit,” “Turn in,” “Upload draft,” “Share to teacher,” and “Post.” They pick the wrong one and their file ends up in a discussion thread instead of the assignment inbox.

Discuss with a partner or reflect in writing: Why do too many similar actions increase mistakes? What is one label change (wording only) that could make the right choice obvious?

Part 3 – Information hierarchy and a simple wireframe (about 5 minutes)

Information hierarchy means the most important idea appears first or looks strongest (title, steps, warnings). Plain-language errors tell you what went wrong and what to try next—not only error codes. Consistent patterns (same word for the same action everywhere) reduce memory load.

Do this:

  1. List three elements you would expect at the top of a “Submit homework” screen (e.g. assignment title, due date, instructions summary).
  2. Sketch a simple wireframe in the box below. Draw boxes and labels only (no art). Show one screen with a title, one main button, and one short error message area.

Part 4 – Constraints designers work with (about 5 minutes)

Constraints are limits the product must respect: accessibility (readable text size, captions, not relying on color alone), language (clear vocabulary for many reading levels), low bandwidth (heavy video vs text option), and error recovery (undo, try again, contact support path). Ignoring constraints creates products that look powerful but exclude people or break under stress.

Part 5 – Notifications, due dates, and tradeoffs (about 5 minutes)

Designers often balance speed (fewer steps) with clarity (more confirmations), and simplicity (few options) with power (advanced settings). Notifications can remind—or overwhelm. Due date displays can stress—or help plan—depending on wording, timing, and defaults.

Real-life scenario: A learning app sends three reminders the night before a deadline: one push notification, one email, and one in-app banner that blocks the screen until dismissed. Some students say they never miss a date; others say they turn off the app entirely.
