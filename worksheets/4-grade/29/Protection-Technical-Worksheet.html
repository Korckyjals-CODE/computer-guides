Protection: Technical Worksheet

Grade 4 · Worksheet · About 25 minutes
Work with pen and paper. Use color pencils if you like. You do not need a computer or the internet.

How to use this worksheet
  • Do the parts in order from top to bottom.
  • Each part has a time so you know how long to spend.
  • Read every instruction before you write. You can do this on your own.

Part 1 – What you will learn (about 3 minutes)

What you will learn: Protection means keeping your devices and your information safe. In this worksheet you will learn about passwords, lock screens, secure websites, and safe habits. You will find out what to protect and how.

Do this:

  1. Name 2 things you use a computer or tablet for (e.g. homework, games, email).
  2. In one sentence, why is it important to protect your device and information?

Part 2 – Passwords and lock screens (about 5 minutes)

A password is like a key to your accounts. A strong password has letters, numbers, and is hard for others to guess (e.g. Sunshine7!). A weak password is easy to guess (e.g. 12345 or password). A lock screen keeps your device private when you step away. Always lock your screen when you leave your device.

Real-life scenario: Your friend leaves their tablet unlocked on their desk while they go to the bathroom. When they come back, someone has changed their wallpaper and opened their games. They feel upset.

Discuss with a partner or think: Why does locking the screen help? What could have been worse if someone had more time?

Do this:

  1. Circle the stronger password: abc123     Mountain9!     111111     MyDog2024
  2. Write one rule you will follow about locking your device.

Part 3 – Secure websites (about 5 minutes)

A secure website uses a special connection so others cannot easily see what you type. Look for a lock icon (padlock) near the web address. Trusted sites (e.g. school, known games, your bank) usually have it. If a website looks strange or asks for your password in a pop-up, do not click. Ask a grown-up first.

Scenario: A pop-up appears: "Your device is infected! Click here to fix it now!" The message looks urgent and scary.

Critical thinking: Why might someone fake a warning like that? (Hint: What do they want you to do? Is clicking always safe?)

Do this: What would you do if you saw that pop-up? Write 1–2 sentences.

Part 4 – What to protect (about 5 minutes)

Some information is personal. We share it only with people we trust (like family or teachers). We never share it with strangers online. Think before you type or click.

Part 5 – Protection habits (about 5 minutes)

Good protection habits keep you safer over time. Examples: Install updates when your device asks (they often fix security problems). Keep a backup of important files (a copy in another place, like the cloud or a USB drive). Do not click links or download files from people you do not know.

Do this: Draw or list 3 protection habits you will use for your device. You can draw simple pictures or write short phrases.

Part 6 – Check what you learned (about 2 minutes)

Checklist. Put a ✓ in the box when it is true for you.

  • □ I know what protection means (keeping devices and information safe).
  • □ I know the difference between a strong and weak password.
  • □ I know to lock my screen when I step away.
  • □ I know to look for the lock icon on websites and not to click fake warnings.
  • □ I know which information to protect and how.

When all boxes are checked, you have reviewed the main ideas from this worksheet.
