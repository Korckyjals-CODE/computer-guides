Protection: Social Worksheet

Grade 4 · Worksheet · About 25 minutes
Work with pen and paper. Use color pencils if you like. You do not need a computer or the internet.

Name: Grade/Section: Date:
How to use this worksheet
  • Do the parts in order from top to bottom.
  • Each part has a time so you know how long to spend.
  • Read every instruction before you write. You can do this on your own.

Part 1 – What you will learn (about 3 minutes)

What you will learn: Protection is about choices and trust. In this worksheet you will learn when to share information and when not to, who to trust, and how to help others stay safe. Your choices online matter—for you and for people around you.

Do this:

  1. Name one person you trust with your personal information (e.g. mom, dad, teacher).
  2. In one sentence, why do we choose who to trust with our information?

Part 2 – Who can you trust? (about 5 minutes)

Trusted people are those you know in real life and who care about you—like family, teachers, or close friends. Strangers online might not be who they say they are. Even friends can accidentally share your information with others. When someone asks for personal details online, ask yourself: Do I know them in real life? Would I tell them this in person?

Real-life scenario: Someone in a game chat says "I'm a kid too! What school do you go to? We could be friends."

Discuss with a partner or think: Why is sharing your school name risky? What could happen if the person is not really a kid?

Do this: Circle what you would do: Tell them my school     Don't answer and tell a grown-up     Ask them their name first

Part 3 – When friends ask for your password (about 5 minutes)

Even if a friend means well, sharing your password weakens your protection. Your friend might accidentally tell someone else, use your account on an unsafe device, or have their own account hacked—and then your password could be stolen too. Saying no does not mean you are a bad friend. It means you care about staying safe.

Scenario: Your friend says "I'm stuck on level 10! Can you give me your game password so I can try it on your account? I promise I won't tell anyone."

Critical thinking: What could go wrong if you shared your password? (Hint: Think about who else might see it, or what could happen to your account.)

Do this: Write one way you could say no kindly.

Part 4 – Protecting others (about 5 minutes)

You can help family and friends stay safe too. Tell a grown-up if you see something strange. Do not spread rumours or share things that could hurt someone. When a friend has a problem online, you can listen and suggest they tell a trusted adult.

Do this: Match each situation to the helpful or safe response. Use the word bank.

SituationHelpful or safe response
Your sibling gets an email: "You won! Click here!"
A friend says "Someone was mean to me online."
Someone shares a rumour about a classmate online.

Word bank: Tell a grown-up together   Don't spread it; tell a grown-up if it's serious   Tell them not to click; ask a grown-up first

Part 5 – Your choices matter (about 5 minutes)

Saying no to sharing a password or personal information can feel awkward. But you can do it in a kind way. You do not have to share to be a good friend. Your choices also set an example—when you protect yourself, others may learn from you.

Do this: Write 2 ways to say no when someone asks for your password. Example: "I'd rather not share, but we can play together at my house."

Part 6 – Check what you learned (about 2 minutes)

Checklist. Put a ✓ in the box when it is true for you.

  • □ I know who to trust with my information and who not to.
  • □ I understand why sharing passwords is risky, even with friends.
  • □ I know how to say no kindly when someone asks for my password.
  • □ I know how to help others stay safe (e.g. tell a grown-up, don't spread rumours).
  • □ I know my choices online matter for me and for others.

When all boxes are checked, you have reviewed the main ideas from this worksheet.
