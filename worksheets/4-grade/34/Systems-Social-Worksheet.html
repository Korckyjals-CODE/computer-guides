Part 1 - What you will learn (about 3 minutes)

What you will learn: Systems are not only machines. A social system is a group of people, rules, and actions working together. In this worksheet, you will learn how systems in school and community depend on fairness, communication, and responsibility.

Do this:

  1. Write one social system you know (e.g. classroom routines, library checkout, bus line).
  2. In one sentence, explain why people must follow shared rules in that system.

Part 2 - Roles and rules in a system (about 5 minutes)

In a social system, people have roles (what each person does) and rules (how the system works). If roles are unclear, people get confused. If rules are ignored, the system becomes unfair.

Real-life scenario: In a classroom cleanup system, one student puts away books, another wipes tables, and another checks the floor. One student refuses to do their role, so others have to do extra work.

Discuss with a partner or reflect in writing: How does one person not doing their role affect everyone else?

Part 3 - Fairness and access (about 5 minutes)

A good system gives people fair access. Fair does not always mean exactly the same. It means the rules help everyone participate safely and respectfully.

Scenario: The drinking fountain line has no order. Taller students move ahead, and younger students wait too long.

Critical thinking: What is the difference between "fast for some" and "fair for all"?

Do this: Create 3 rules to make the fountain line system fair.

Part 4 - Communication inside systems (about 5 minutes)

Social systems work best when communication is clear. People need to know what to do, when to do it, and who to ask for help. Poor communication causes mistakes and frustration.

Real-life scenario: The school announces a schedule change, but not all students hear it. Some classes go to the wrong place.

Part 5 - Improving a social system (about 5 minutes)

Social systems can be improved when people observe problems, listen to others, and test better ideas. Improvement should help the group, not only one person.
