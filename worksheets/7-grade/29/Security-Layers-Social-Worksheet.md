## Part 2 – Sharing and trust (about 5 minutes)

Sharing passwords or letting others use your account weakens the **account layer**. Even if you trust a friend, they might accidentally share it, use it on an unsafe device, or have their own account hacked. Once a password is shared, you lose control of who sees it.

**Real-life scenario:** Your friend asks for your Netflix password so they can watch a show. They say "Everyone shares passwords. It's fine." You like your friend and want to help, but you have heard that sharing passwords is risky.

**Discuss with a partner or reflect in writing:** Why is sharing passwords risky? What could go wrong even if you trust your friend?

## Part 3 – Skipping steps (about 5 minutes)

Sometimes we skip security steps for convenience. Ignoring software updates, reusing the same password everywhere, or using public Wi‑Fi for sensitive tasks can weaken layers. Each choice may seem small, but together they create gaps that attackers can use.

**Scenario:** A student has been ignoring the "update your phone" notification for weeks. They use the same password for school, email, and games. One day their school account is hacked. The attacker posts embarrassing messages. Later they find out a game site they used was breached and passwords were leaked.

**Critical thinking:** Who is responsible—the student, the school, or the game company? Could more than one party have done something differently?

## Part 4 – Peer pressure and convenience (about 5 minutes)

Sometimes friends say "Everyone does it" or "It's not a big deal." Choosing security over convenience can feel awkward. But saying no in a clear, kind way protects you and sets a good example. You do not have to share passwords to be a good friend.

**Scenario:** Your group of friends shares one streaming account. They put the password in a group chat. One friend says "Just use it. Nobody cares." You are worried because the password is now visible to everyone in the chat—and anyone who might see their phones.

**Discuss with a partner or reflect in writing:** What are the trade-offs? What do you gain by saying no? What might you lose?

## Part 5 – Helping others stay secure (about 5 minutes)

You can help family and friends stay secure. Teach them simple habits: lock the screen, use strong passwords, do not click suspicious links. If someone gets a strange email or message, encourage them to ask a trusted adult or report it. Security is something we do together.

**Scenario:** Your younger sibling gets an email that says "You won a free tablet! Click here to claim it." The email looks a bit odd. Your sibling is excited and wants to click.
