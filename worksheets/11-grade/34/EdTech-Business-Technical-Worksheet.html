Part 1 – What you will learn (about 3 minutes)

What you will learn: EdTech business looks at learning products as organizations that must design software, win customers, deliver support, and stay trusted. You will compare buyers (who pays) to end users (who logs in daily), contrast B2B (business-to-business, often district or school contracts) with B2C (business-to-consumer, families or students pay directly), and see why implementation, integrations, metrics, and compliance shape what gets built and sold.

Do this:

  1. Name one learning product or company you have heard of (any app, platform, tutoring brand, or device program tied to school).
  2. In one sentence, who do you think pays for it most often—the school district, a school, a teacher, a parent, or the student?

Part 2 – Buyers, users, and sales motion (about 5 minutes)

The buyer signs the contract or enters the credit card. The user actually teaches, grades, or studies with the tool. In B2B EdTech, a district office might buy for many schools; principals or department leads may run a pilot (small trial) before a full purchase. In B2C EdTech, marketing often speaks straight to families or independent learners. Sales teams create demos and answer requests for proposals (RFPs)—long questionnaires districts use to compare vendors fairly.

Real-life scenario: A company sells an AI writing tutor. Version A is priced per school and sold through long district meetings. Version B is sold in an app store with a monthly subscription anyone can buy.

Discuss with a partner or reflect in writing: How might support, training, and product updates differ when the main customer is a district office versus an individual subscriber?

Part 3 – Revenue models and the “whole product” (about 5 minutes)

Revenue models describe how money flows: subscriptions (renew every term or year), site licenses (one price for many seats), freemium (basic free tier, pay for advanced features), or hardware bundles (devices plus software). The whole product includes onboarding guides, help-desk hours, data migration from old systems, and teacher training—not only the login screen students see.

Scenario: A vendor offers the core gradebook free but charges extra for exports, phone support, and advanced reports.

Part 4 – Integrations and implementation risk (about 5 minutes)

Integrations connect products so rosters, grades, or sign-in systems stay in sync (for example, a learning app reading class lists from a central directory). Implementation is the rollout plan: timelines, training sessions, pilot classrooms, and cutover dates when everyone switches. Weak integrations create duplicate accounts, wrong classes, or lost submissions—expensive support tickets for the vendor and lost trust for the school.

Real-life scenario: A science department adopts a new lab simulation mid-year. Rosters never sync; half the students appear in “Period 3” in the LMS but “Period 4” in the lab tool.

Discuss with a partner or reflect in writing: Name two stakeholders (roles or offices) who should be in the room before the switch goes district-wide. Why do they matter?

Part 5 – Metrics, differentiation, and compliance as product requirements (about 5 minutes)

Vendors track product metrics such as weekly active users, assignment completion rates, support backlog, and renewal likelihood. Differentiation explains why a buyer picks this product over another (unique curriculum, local language support, strongest privacy stance). Compliance means meeting laws and policies about student data (for example, rules about consent, deletion, and subprocessors). Buyers increasingly ask for data processing agreements that spell out who may access information and why.

Scenario: Two dashboards look identical to students, but Dashboard X sells aggregated usage patterns to advertisers while Dashboard Y charges higher fees and blocks third-party ads.

Critical thinking: If you were a district technology committee member, what is one question you would ask each vendor about data use before recommending a purchase?
