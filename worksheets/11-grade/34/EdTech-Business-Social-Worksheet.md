## Part 2 – Access, prestige, and the “digital divide” (about 5 minutes)

**Equitable access** is not only devices; it includes quiet space, translation help, assistive tech training, and time to learn new platforms while still doing coursework. When some schools get one-to-one devices and premium tutoring bundles while others patch together old laptops, **opportunity gaps** widen. Marketing that shows only “elite” college acceptances can make families feel they must buy add-ons to keep up.

**Real-life scenario:** Two schools in the same city share a stadium but one receives a donated STEM suite with mentors; the other shares two computer carts for the whole grade.

**Discuss with a partner or reflect in writing:** What is one **public policy or school partnership** (not an individual purchase) that could narrow that gap?

## Part 3 – Commercial pressure inside classrooms (about 5 minutes)

**Freemium** learning apps may show ads, sell add-on reports, or push streaks that keep users opening the app daily. **Upselling** moves families from a free trial to paid tiers with urgency (“last chance pricing”). Students can feel **shame** if they cannot afford extras classmates use. Teachers may face tension between district mandates and what they think is healthy for teens.

**Scenario:** A literacy app awards flashy coins for rapid responses; the leaderboard is visible to the whole class. Students with slower reading speeds appear at the bottom every week.

## Part 4 – Surveillance, trust, and student agency (about 5 minutes)

Some EdTech includes **proctoring**, keystroke logs, location checks, or always-on cameras. Supporters say it reduces cheating; critics say it can harm **privacy**, **mental health**, and **trust**, especially for students already watched more closely because of bias. **Student agency** means learners understand what is collected, can ask questions, and have paths to fix mistakes (like a false flag).

**Real-life scenario:** A dashboard emails parents every time a student’s tab loses focus during remote practice—without explaining internet glitches or siblings walking into the room.

**Discuss with a partner or reflect in writing:** When might **aggregate** information (trends for a whole class) serve learning better than **individual alerts** sent home automatically?

## Part 5 – Global labor, sustainability, and community voice (about 5 minutes)

EdTech depends on **global supply chains** for devices, **content moderators**, **customer support agents**, and **data centers** that use energy and water. Benefits and harms are uneven across countries. **Community voice** includes student councils, parent associations, unions, and local journalists asking who profits from student data and who answers when systems fail.

**Real-life scenario:** A company closes a regional support office; schools wait weeks for fixes during exam season while chatbots repeat unhelpful scripts.
