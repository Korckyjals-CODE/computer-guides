## Part 1 – What you will learn (about 3 minutes)

**What you will learn:** Many schools use a **learning platform** (sometimes called an **LMS**, short for *learning management system*). It is like a **digital classroom** where announcements, lessons, files, and assignments can live in one organized place. In this worksheet you will learn how these systems are built in simple terms: who uses them, what “rooms” they have, and how work moves from *assigned* to *turned in* to *feedback*.

**Do this:**

## Part 2 – Who does what on a learning platform? (about 5 minutes)

People have different **roles**. A **teacher** (or instructor) usually creates the course space, posts announcements, adds materials, sets due dates, and reviews student work. A **student** opens lessons, reads instructions, uploads or completes work, and reads messages or grades. The **system** (the software) keeps lists, dates, and files in order—it does not replace good communication between people.

## Part 3 – Main “areas” inside a typical platform (about 5 minutes)

Most learning platforms split the screen into **sections** you can visit. Common examples:

| Section | What it often holds |
|---------|---------------------|
| **Home / Stream** | Latest news, reminders, and new posts |
| **Assignments / Tasks** | What you must do, instructions, due dates |
| **Materials / Files** | PDFs, links, videos, or handouts |
| **Calendar** | Dates for tests, projects, and meetings |
| **Grades / Progress** | Scores or “completed / not completed” (depends on the school) |

## Part 4 – How work flows: assign → submit → feedback (about 5 minutes)

A simple **workflow** is a path work follows:

1. **Assigned** — The teacher publishes a task with instructions and a due date.  
2. **Submitted** — The student sends or uploads the work before or on the due date.  
3. **Feedback** — The teacher (or sometimes a rubric) returns comments, a score, or “revise and resubmit.”

**Notifications** (little alerts or badges) remind you that something changed—but they can be wrong if you misread them, so you still check the **Assignments** list.

## Part 5 – Data the platform shows (about 5 minutes)

A platform stores **information** (data) such as: file names, dates, “turned in on time / late,” quiz scores, and discussion posts. You do not need to know programming—you need to know **what the labels mean** (e.g. *Missing* vs *Submitted*) and that **two screens** might show the same fact in different words.
