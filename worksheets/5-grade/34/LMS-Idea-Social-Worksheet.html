Part 1 – What you will learn (about 3 minutes)

What you will learn: When classes use a learning platform, people still make choices about respect, honesty, privacy, and kindness. This worksheet helps you think about how your words and actions affect classmates and teachers online—not about clicking buttons, but about being a good digital citizen in spaces where school work and conversations happen.

Do this:

  1. Write one goal you have for yourself in online class spaces this year (one short sentence).

  2. Circle the word that fits best for you right now: I feel confident / curious / nervous about posting in class discussions.

Part 2 – Respect in comments and replies (about 5 minutes)

Tone is how your message sounds to others. ALL CAPS can sound like shouting. Short replies like “Whatever” can sound rude, even if you did not mean that. Clear, calm sentences help teachers and peers understand you.

Part 3 – Honesty with your work (about 5 minutes)

On a learning platform, it can be tempting to copy a classmate’s file, share answers in a private chat, or let someone else log in as you. Those choices break trust and often break school rules. Honest work means the ideas and answers you submit are fairly yours, with allowed help (like your teacher or family explaining a concept, not giving you every answer).

Personal information includes your full name with address, phone numbers, passwords, family payment details, and embarrassing stories about others. Class spaces are school spaces, not a public billboard.

Part 5 – Asking for help and including others (about 5 minutes)

Good help-seeking uses the channel your teacher prefers (e.g. a private message to the teacher, a “question” thread, or speaking after class). Including others means not making jokes at someone’s expense and inviting quiet classmates into group work when roles are shared fairly.

Scenario — Side chat pressure

During an online discussion, a few students start a side joke about another student’s answer. You are not the one who started it, but you see the thread.

Do this:

  1. List two actions that help (examples: defend the classmate kindly, change the subject, tell a trusted adult, remind others of class rules).
  2. Write a short reply (1–2 sentences) you could post publicly to move the conversation back to the lesson without attacking anyone.


Part 6 – Check what you learned (about 2 minutes)

Checklist. Put a ✓ in the box when it is true for you.

  • □ I can describe why tone and respectful language matter in class platforms.
  • □ I know what honest work means and why copying or shared logins are wrong.
  • □ I can name types of personal information that should stay private at school.
  • □ I have ideas for asking for help in a clear, appropriate way.
  • □ I thought through a peer-pressure scenario and a positive response.

When all boxes are checked, you have reviewed the main ideas from this worksheet.
