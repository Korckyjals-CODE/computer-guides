Part 1 – What you will learn (about 3 minutes)

What you will learn: Education technology (EdTech) is the hardware and software schools use to organize courses, turn in work, give grades and feedback, and communicate. A learning platform is often the “hub” where those pieces connect. In this worksheet you will learn how a platform differs from a single app, how accounts and roles work at a basic level, what data is created when you learn online, and how accessibility and security choices protect learners.

Do this:

  1. Name one school system or app you have used for learning (course page, gradebook, chat, video meeting—any example).
  2. In one sentence, what do you want to understand better about how that system works behind the screen?

Part 2 – Platform vs single app (about 5 minutes)

A single-purpose learning app usually does one job well (flashcards, graphing, language drills). A learning platform connects many jobs in one place: announcements, lessons, assignments, a gradebook, calendars, and sometimes video or chat. Data can flow between parts so teachers do not retype the same roster in ten tools.

Real-life scenario: Your school posts the syllabus in the platform, you submit an essay there, you check feedback in the same course, and you see the due date on the calendar—all without opening five unrelated websites.

Discuss with a partner or reflect in writing: When is a simple app enough? When does a whole platform make school life easier or harder? Give one reason for each.

Part 3 – Accounts, roles, and sign-in (about 5 minutes)

Your account is your digital identity at school: it ties your name to your work and permissions. Roles set what someone is allowed to do. A student role usually submits work and sees their own grades. A teacher role can create assignments and give feedback. An administrator (or IT staff) keeps accounts safe and fixes system-wide settings. Many schools use single sign-on: one school username and password opens several approved tools so you are not juggling unsafe duplicates.

Scenario: A pop-up asks you to log in to “ClassHelper Pro” with your personal email, not your school account. The logo looks almost like a tool your teacher mentioned.

Critical thinking: Why might using your school account only on official school pages be safer? What is one question you could ask a teacher or librarian before you enter a password anywhere new?

Part 4 – Data, visibility, and backups (about 5 minutes)

When you use EdTech, the system creates data: files you upload, quiz answers, timestamps, discussion posts, and sometimes logs (records that an action happened). Visibility rules decide who can see what—only you, your teacher, the class, or IT for support. Backups are saved copies so work can be recovered if a server fails or someone deletes the wrong file. Good systems limit permissions so people only access what their job needs.

Real-life scenario: You post a rough draft in a class discussion by mistake. A classmate screenshots your unfinished work and shares it in a group chat.

Discuss with a partner or reflect in writing: Which mix of tool settings and class norms could reduce harm in situations like this? Name one setting or habit (e.g. private draft folder, ask before screenshot).

Part 5 – Accessibility and security hygiene (about 5 minutes)

Accessibility means more people can use the tool: captions on videos, readable fonts and contrast, headings for screen readers, and designs that do not rely only on color to mean “correct” or “wrong.” Security hygiene includes unique strong passwords (or school-approved password managers), not sharing logins, and spotting phishing: fake messages that rush you to click a dangerous link or “confirm” your password.

Scenario: An email says your grade will be deleted unless you log in within an hour. The link spelling is slightly wrong (sch00l-login.net).

Critical thinking: List two clues that this message might be phishing, not a real school notice.
