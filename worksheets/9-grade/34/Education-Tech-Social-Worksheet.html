Part 1 – What you will learn (about 3 minutes)

What you will learn: Education technology changes how we learn together: who can join, who feels confident, and how we treat each other when screens are involved. In this worksheet you will think about fair access, privacy and respect, communication norms in digital classes, honesty with digital help, and well-being when notifications and comparisons never stop.

Part 2 – Equity and access (about 5 minutes)

Equitable access means learners can reach lessons, understand instructions, and participate even when home Wi-Fi, devices, quiet space, or language support varies. Flexibility (deadlines that allow upload retries, offline options, captions, translated materials) can shrink gaps—but only if schools and classmates understand the pressures behind the screen.

Real-life scenario: Two students work on the same video assignment. One has fiber internet and a quiet room. The other shares a phone hotspot with siblings and has to babysit after school. The platform shows both submissions as “late” for the same hour.

Discuss with a partner or reflect in writing: What is one policy or class habit (not blame toward a person) that could make due times fairer when home situations differ?

Part 3 – Privacy, respect, and screenshots (about 5 minutes)

Learning tools can show grades, drafts, health forms, or family names. Consent matters: ask before you photograph or screenshot someone’s work. Leaks happen when files are forwarded without thinking. Schools and companies may collect usage data (time on task, clicks) to improve tools—learners deserve clear, age-appropriate explanations of what is collected and why.

Scenario: Someone posts a funny clip of a classmate’s accidental hot-mic moment from a video class to social media. The classmate is embarrassed and asks for it to be taken down. People say, “It was just a joke.”

Critical thinking: Who is affected beyond the person in the clip? What are two actions that show respect (online or offline) after a mistake like this?

Part 4 – Participation norms and honesty with digital help (about 5 minutes)

Participation norms keep digital spaces workable: stay on topic, avoid pile-ons, credit peers’ ideas, and use accessible language (explain slang, avoid mocking typos). Academic integrity means your teachers can trust that the work reflects your thinking. When generative tools draft text or code for you, school rules may require you to label help received, show your own analysis, or not use them on certain tasks—follow your school’s policy even if a tool is easy to open.

Real-life scenario: A group chat shares “perfect answers” copied from a help site during a take-home quiz. Someone says, “Everyone does it; the platform cannot prove anything.”

Discuss with a partner or reflect in writing: How does this choice affect trust between students and teachers? How does it affect students who refuse to cheat?

Part 5 – Well-being, attention, and comparison (about 5 minutes)

Notifications pull attention away from reading and thinking. Social comparison spikes when grades, streaks, or profile stats are always visible. Breaks, sleep, and offline friendships are not “extras”—they support memory and mood. You can advocate for sensible norms: silent study modes, agreed chat hours, or celebrating improvement—not only speed.
