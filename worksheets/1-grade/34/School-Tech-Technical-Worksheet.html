School Tech: Technical Worksheet

Grade 1 · Worksheet · About 25 minutes
Work with pen and paper. Use color pencils if you like. You do not need a computer or the internet.

Name: Grade/Section: Date:
How to use this worksheet
  • Do the parts in order from top to bottom.
  • Each part has a time so you know how long to spend.
  • Read every instruction before you write. You can do this on your own.

Part 1 – What you will learn (about 3 minutes)

What you will learn: School technology means the computers, tablets, screens, and other tools your class uses to learn. In this worksheet you will learn names for common tools, what they help you do, and how to use them in a safe, careful way.

Do this:

  1. Write one tool you have seen at school (e.g. tablet, computer, big screen).
  2. Circle where you have used or seen school tech: In the classroom   In the library   Somewhere else (write where):

Part 2 – Tools at school (about 5 minutes)

At school you might see a computer, a tablet, a screen (sometimes called a smart board), headphones, or a mouse. Some screens work when you touch them. These tools can show pictures, letters, sounds, and learning games.

Real-life scenario: During a class story on the screen, the picture suddenly turns black. You are not sure if it is broken. What is a safe first step? (Hint: Grown-ups at school can help.)

Discuss with a partner or think: Why is it smart to ask a teacher before you press many buttons on class devices?

Do this:

  1. Circle three things that are school technology: Pencil   Tablet   Erasable marker   Headphones   Lunch tray
  2. Draw one school technology tool you know in the box below.

Part 3 – What school tech helps us do (about 5 minutes)

School technology can show words and pictures. It can play sounds so the class can listen. It can help you practice reading, counting, or drawing ideas. The class often follows the teacher’s plan for when and how to use each tool.

Real-life scenario: The teacher asks everyone to listen to a quiet story on tablets. The person next to you forgot their headphones. The sound is a little loud.

Discuss with a partner or think: Why do people sometimes use headphones at school?

Critical thinking: Which choice is usually better for not bothering neighbors during quiet work—a loud speaker for everyone or headphones? Write one sentence to explain your answer.

Do this: Circle the better choice for quiet work time: Everyone uses loud sound   Use headphones when you can

Part 4 – Taking care of school tech (about 5 minutes)

School tools need gentle hands. Do not yank cords or cables. Do not run or swing bags near screens. If something looks loose or on the floor, tell a teacher instead of pulling it yourself. Treat devices like important classroom materials.

Real-life scenario: You see a cord sticking out from the computer cart. Your friend says, “Pull it—it might be stuck!”

Critical thinking: What could go wrong if someone yanks a cord? Write one idea.

Do this: Match each situation to what you should do. Write the word from the word bank on the line.

Part 5 – When something seems wrong (about 5 minutes)

Sometimes the screen freezes, the sound is too loud, or you are not sure which icon to press. Stop and ask your teacher (or another grown-up at school). That is part of learning to use technology well.

Do this:

  1. Fill in: “If the tablet screen will not change when I tap, I will ______________.”
  2. Write or draw the name of one grown-up at school who can help you with school tech (e.g. your teacher, librarian).

Part 6 – Check what you learned (about 2 minutes)

Checklist. Put a ✓ in the box when it is true for you.

  • □ I can name at least one school technology tool.
  • □ I know school tech can show pictures, sounds, and learning activities.
  • □ I know to use gentle hands and not yank cords.
  • □ I know to ask a teacher when something looks unsafe or confuses me.

When all boxes are checked, you have reviewed the main ideas from this worksheet.
