Part 1 – What you will learn (about 3 minutes)

What you will learn: Digital learning means learning with digital tools—lessons, practice, stories, or quizzes that use a screen, speakers, or headphones. The tool stores information so you can read it, hear it, or answer questions in order. In this worksheet you will learn what makes a digital lesson different from only paper, what input and output mean, and how feedback and progress help you learn.

Do this:

  1. Have you ever learned something using a screen at school or at home? Circle: Yes or No
  2. Name one digital tool you have seen used for learning (e.g. tablet lesson, video with a teacher, practice questions on a screen).

Part 2 – Parts of a digital lesson (about 5 minutes)

Many digital lessons have the same kinds of pieces. There is a title or topic so you know what it is about. There are instructions that tell you what to do. There is practice—questions or activities you try yourself. There is feedback: the tool may tell you if an answer is correct, show a hint, or play a sound so you know what happened. Progress means how far you have gone (like a bookmark: "you stopped here last time").

Part 3 – Input and output (about 5 minutes)

Input is what you send to the tool: you tap, click, type letters, speak into a mic, or choose an answer. Output is what the tool sends back to you: words on the screen, pictures, videos, or sound through speakers or headphones.

Real-life scenario: In math practice on a tablet, you tap the answer "12" (input). The screen flashes green and says "Nice work!" (output). Then the next question appears (output).

Discuss with a partner or reflect in writing: Why is it helpful that the tool can respond right away after your input? What might happen if you could not see or hear the output clearly?

Part 4 – Staying organized (about 5 minutes)

Digital lessons and saved work need good names and a clear order—just like putting a label on a folder in your desk. If every file is called "Untitled" or "Homework," it is hard to find yours later. If activities are in a sensible order (lesson 1, then lesson 2), you know what to open next.

Scenario: Two students save their writing. One names the file My_Dog_Story_May. The other saves as Document1. A week later the teacher asks everyone to open their dog story. Which student will probably find their work faster? Why?

Critical thinking: What is one rule you could follow when you name your own digital work so you can find it again?

Part 5 – How a simple digital lesson flows (about 5 minutes)

A digital learning system is a group of parts that work together: you, the device, the lesson, and saved progress. The flow is often: open the lesson → read or listen → do practice → get feedback → stop or continue later from the same place.
