Digital Learning: Social Worksheet

Grade 3 · Worksheet · About 25 minutes
Work with pen and paper. Use color pencils if you like. You do not need a computer or the internet.

Name: Grade/Section: Date:
How to use this worksheet
  • Do the parts in order from top to bottom.
  • Each part has a time so you know how long to spend.
  • Read every instruction before you write. You can do this on your own.

Part 1 – What you will learn (about 3 minutes)

What you will learn: Digital learning means learning together or alone using screens, headphones, or other school technology. How we act still matters: we show respect, take turns, and protect privacy. In this worksheet you will learn about fair sharing of devices, respect for classmates' screens and work, kind communication in digital spaces, and healthy habits while learning.

Do this:

  1. Have you ever shared a device or a learning station with someone else? Circle: Yes or No
  2. Write one word that describes how you want others to treat you when you are learning with technology (e.g. fair, kind, patient).

Part 2 – Fair turns and time (about 5 minutes)

When devices or seats at a computer table are shared, taking turns keeps learning fair. A timer, a list, or a teacher's plan can help everyone know when it is their time. Rushing someone or skipping a turn breaks trust.

Real-life scenario: Four students have 20 minutes with two tablets. The group agrees: each pair gets 10 minutes, then they switch. One student tries to keep the tablet an extra five minutes because "I am not done yet."

Discuss with a partner or reflect in writing: How does keeping extra time affect the other pair? What is a fair way to solve "I am not done yet" without stealing someone else's turn?

Do this: Write two agreements your class could use when sharing devices.

Part 3 – Privacy and respect on screens (about 5 minutes)

A classmate's screen can show private things: their answers, their name, their photo, or their score. You should ask before you look or touch someone else's device. Do not read their messages over their shoulder unless they invite you. Helping is great—ask first ("Can I show you this button?") instead of grabbing.

Scenario: During silent reading on tablets, you finish early. You lean over to see your friend's game because it looks fun. They did not say you could watch.

Critical thinking: Why can leaning over feel different from looking at a poster on the wall? What could you say or do instead next time?

Do this: For each action, write Kind or Not kind.

ActionKind or Not kind?
Asking, "May I look at your screen to help?"
Closing someone else's tab without asking
Looking away if you see a password on their screen

Part 4 – Kind and clear messages (about 5 minutes)

Sometimes digital learning includes messages to a teacher or classmates: a short comment, a chat box, or a question field. Use the same respect as face to face: greet if it fits, use polite words, stay on topic, and do not type in ALL CAPS (it can sound like shouting).

Real-life scenario: A student types "this is stupid" in the class chat because the activity is hard. Other students feel upset or distracted.

Discuss with a partner or reflect in writing: What is a kinder way to ask for help when something feels hard? Who could you ask?

Do this: Rewrite each message to be kind and clear.

Not helpfulYour kind version
"I hate this."
"Hurry up!!!"

Part 5 – Focus, help, and healthy breaks (about 5 minutes)

Learning with technology works best when we focus and avoid distracting others (quiet voices, sensible volume in headphones). If you are stuck, ask a teacher or trusted adult for help. Never share passwords or try to log in as someone else—say no and tell a grown-up if someone asks for your password.

Short breaks for eyes and body are important: look away from the screen, stretch, or stand when the teacher says it is time. That helps you stay ready to learn.

Scenario: A friend asks for your login so they can "just check the answers." You feel pressured because they say they will not be your friend otherwise.

Critical thinking: What is the safest choice? What could you say? Who should know about pressure like this?

Do this: List two healthy things you can do during a learning break (e.g. stretch, drink water).

Part 6 – Check what you learned (about 2 minutes)

Checklist. Put a ✓ in the box when it is true for you.

  • □ I understand why taking turns and fair time matter when devices are shared.
  • □ I know to ask before I look at or touch someone else's screen or work.
  • □ I can write or choose kind, clear messages in digital learning spaces.
  • □ I know to ask adults for help and not to share passwords or log in as others.
  • □ I can name healthy break habits that support focus and well-being.

When all boxes are checked, you have reviewed the main ideas from this worksheet.
