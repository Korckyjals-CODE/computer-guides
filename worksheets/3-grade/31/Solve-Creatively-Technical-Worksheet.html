Solve Creatively: Technical Worksheet

Grade 3 · Worksheet · About 25 minutes
Work with pen and paper. Use color pencils if you like. You do not need a computer or the internet.

How to use this worksheet
  • Do the parts in order from top to bottom.
  • Each part has a time so you know how long to spend.
  • Read every instruction before you write. You can do this on your own.

Part 1 – What you will learn (about 3 minutes)

What you will learn: Creative problem-solving means thinking of different ways to fix a problem or reach a goal. You will learn steps to solve creatively: break the problem into smaller parts, think of many ideas (called brainstorming), try them out, and learn from what does not work.

Do this:

  1. Have you ever solved a problem in a creative way? Circle: Yes or No
  2. Name one problem you could solve creatively (e.g. organizing your toys, making a new game, fixing something that broke).

Part 2 – Steps to solve creatively (about 5 minutes)

When you face a problem, you can follow steps. First, understand the problem—what do you need to fix or do? Second, brainstorm—think of as many ideas as you can, even silly ones. Third, try one idea. Fourth, see what happens. If it does not work, try another idea. That is called trial and error.

Do this: Match each step to what you do. Use the word bank.

Part 3 – Brainstorming and trying ideas (about 5 minutes)

Brainstorming means thinking of lots of ideas without saying "that won't work." At first, every idea is okay. Later you pick the best ones. When you try an idea, you learn what works and what does not.

Real-life scenario: The class needs to organize the library. Books are mixed up and hard to find. The teacher asks: "How could we organize them?" Students brainstorm: by color, by size, by topic, by first letter of the title, by how much we like them.

Discuss with a partner or reflect in writing: Which idea do you think would work best? Why? Can you combine two ideas?

Do this: List 3 ways you could organize the library. Write any ideas—even funny ones!


Part 4 – Trial and error (about 5 minutes)

Trial and error means trying something, seeing if it works, and trying again if it does not. When you build a tower with blocks and it falls, you try a different way. Each try teaches you something.

Scenario: You are building a tower with blocks. You put the big blocks on top. The tower keeps falling. You try putting the big blocks on the bottom. Now it stands!

Critical thinking: Why do we try again instead of giving up? What do we learn when something does not work?

Do this: Draw a tower that would stand (or write the order: bottom first, then top).
