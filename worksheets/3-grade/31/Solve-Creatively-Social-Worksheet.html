Solve Creatively: Social Worksheet

Grade 3 · Worksheet · About 25 minutes
Work with pen and paper. Use color pencils if you like. You do not need a computer or the internet.

How to use this worksheet
  • Do the parts in order from top to bottom.
  • Each part has a time so you know how long to spend.
  • Read every instruction before you write. You can do this on your own.

Part 1 – What you will learn (about 3 minutes)

What you will learn: Creativity is not only for you. We can use it with others. When we share ideas, listen, and build on each other's thoughts, we find better solutions. In this worksheet you will learn about sharing ideas, how creative solutions help people, and respecting different ideas.

Do this:

  1. Have you ever shared an idea with a friend or group? Circle: Yes or No
  2. Name one way creative thinking can help other people.

Part 2 – Sharing ideas with others (about 5 minutes)

When we work with others, we share ideas. Listening to someone else's idea can give us new thoughts. One idea can lead to another. When we build on ideas (add to them or combine them), we often get better solutions.

Real-life scenario: A group is designing a poster for a school event. One student says, "Let's use bright colors." Another says, "And we could add stars!" A third says, "We could make the stars in the shape of our school logo." Each idea builds on the last.

Discuss with a partner or reflect in writing: How did each idea help the next? What if only one person had shared their idea?

Do this: Fill in the table. For each situation, write how you could build on someone's idea.

Part 3 – Creative solutions that help people (about 5 minutes)

Creative thinking can help people at home, at school, and in the community. When we see a problem and think of a new way to fix it, we help others. Even a small creative idea can make someone's day better.

Scenario: A classmate forgot their lunch. They are sad. You think of a creative idea: you could split your sandwich with them, or the class could each share a little bit of their snack so everyone has something.

Critical thinking: Which idea do you think is better? Why? Can you think of another creative way to help?

Part 4 – Respecting different ideas (about 5 minutes)

Everyone has different ideas. That is okay! When two people have different ideas for the same problem, we do not have to pick one. We can combine them or take the best parts of each. We listen and say "that's interesting" or "I had not thought of that" instead of "that's wrong."

Scenario: Two friends want to decorate the classroom. One wants balloons. One wants streamers. They argue. A third friend says, "What if we use both? Balloons by the door and streamers on the walls."

Discuss with a partner or reflect in writing: Why is combining ideas sometimes better than choosing only one? How did the third friend help?

Do this: Write 2 kind things you could say when someone has a different idea from you.

Part 5 – Creativity when people disagree (about 5 minutes)

Sometimes people disagree. Creativity can help! Instead of arguing, we can think of a new way that includes everyone. For example, if friends cannot agree on a game, we could take turns, mix two games, or invent a new one together.

Real-life scenario: Three friends want to play. One wants tag. One wants hide-and-seek. One wants to draw. They cannot agree. Someone suggests: "What if we play tag first, then hide-and-seek, then draw? We take turns choosing."

Critical thinking: How does taking turns solve the problem? Can you think of another creative way to decide?

Do this: Think of a time when you or someone you know used creativity to help people get along. Write 1–2 sentences.

Part 6 – Check what you learned (about 2 minutes)

Checklist. Put a ✓ in the box when it is true for you.

  • □ I know that everyone has different ideas and that is okay.
  • □ I can think of ways to combine ideas when people disagree.
  • □ I can think of a time creativity helped people get along.
