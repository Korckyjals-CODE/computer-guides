## Part 1 – What you will learn (about 3 minutes)

**What you will learn:** A **learning tool** is something you use to learn. It can be a **book**, a **whiteboard**, a **tablet**, or a **computer** at school. In this worksheet you will learn how learning tools show you lessons, how they give **feedback** (like “good job” or “try again”), and how to compare different tools for the same job.

## Part 2 – What counts as a learning tool? (about 5 minutes)

A learning tool helps you **practice**, **read**, **listen**, or **solve problems**. Paper and pencils are learning tools too. A **digital learning tool** is one that uses a **screen** or **sound** from a device (like a tablet or computer at school).

**Real-life scenario:** Leo needs to practice spelling words. He can use paper and pencil, or a spelling activity on a tablet. Both are learning tools.

**Discuss with a partner or think:** Why might a teacher offer more than one way to practice?

## Part 3 – How a tool “talks” to you (about 5 minutes)

Many digital learning tools use **pictures**, **words**, **sound**, and **questions**. You send something to the tool when you **tap**, **click**, **type**, or **speak** (if your class uses that). The tool sends something back when it **shows** a new screen, **plays** a sound, or **tells** you if you are correct.

**Scenario:** The screen shows a picture of three apples and asks, “How many?” You tap the number 3. The tool beeps and shows a smile. What did you send? What did the tool send back?

**Critical thinking:** Why is it helpful when a tool shows you the **next step** instead of skipping ahead?

## Part 4 – Steps and feedback (about 5 minutes)

Good lessons go in **order**: first read or listen, then try, then check. **Feedback** means the tool tells you if you are on the right track (like a green check, stars, or “try again”). Following steps helps you learn better than skipping around.

**Real-life scenario:** Sofia’s class tool says “Step 1: Read.” She wants to jump to the game. Her teacher says, “Do Step 1 first.” Why might Step 1 matter?

## Part 5 – Compare two tools (about 5 minutes)

The **same job** can be done with different tools. A **book** can help you read a story slowly and draw on the page. A **screen tool** can read aloud, show moving pictures, or check your answers quickly. Each has strengths.

**Discuss with a partner or think:** When might a **book** be a better learning tool for you? When might a **screen tool** be better? There is no single right answer for everyone.

**Do this:**
