Cyber Strategy: Social Worksheet

Grade 12 · Worksheet · About 25 minutes
Work with pen and paper. Use color pencils if you like. You do not need a computer or the internet.

Name: Grade/Section: Date:
How to use this worksheet
  • Do the parts in order from top to bottom.
  • Each part has a time so you know how long to spend.
  • Read every instruction before you write. You can do this on your own.

Part 1 – What you will learn (about 3 minutes)

What you will learn: A cyber strategy is not only about technology. It also involves people, policies, culture, and ethics. In this worksheet you will learn about the social side of cyber strategy: how human behaviour affects security, who is responsible for protecting data, and what ethical choices we face online.

Do this:

  1. In one sentence, what is the “social” side of cyber strategy?

  2. Name two social elements of cyber strategy that you will learn in this worksheet.

Part 2 – Human factors in security (about 5 minutes)

Human factors in security mean how people’s behaviour affects safety. People make mistakes, trust too easily, or ignore warnings. Phishing is when attackers trick people into clicking links or giving passwords by pretending to be someone trustworthy. Training and awareness help, but they are not enough by themselves.

Real-life scenario: An employee at a company receives an email that looks like it is from the IT department. It says “Your password expires in 24 hours. Click here to update.” The employee clicks, enters their password, and within hours the company’s systems are breached. Customer data is stolen.

Do this:

  1. List 2 things the employee could have done differently to avoid falling for the phishing email.

  2. Discuss with a partner or reflect in writing: What could the organization do differently to reduce the chance of this happening? (Think beyond blaming the employee.)

Part 3 – Policy and responsibility (about 5 minutes)

Who is responsible when something goes wrong? Individuals (e.g. using strong passwords, not sharing credentials), organizations (e.g. training, policies, technical controls), and governments (e.g. laws, standards) all play a role. Responsibility is often shared.

Scenario: A student’s school account is hacked. The attacker posts embarrassing messages. The student used the same password on several sites, including one that was breached. The school did not require two-factor authentication.

Do this: Fill in the table. For each party, write one thing they could have done to reduce the risk.

The student
The school
(Optional) A company whose site was breached

Critical thinking: Who do you think bears more responsibility in this case—the student or the school? In one sentence, explain why.

Part 4 – Ethical dilemmas (about 5 minutes)

Ethics in cyber security are about what is right or wrong when we use or protect technology. Sometimes there is no clear answer.

Scenario: You discover a serious vulnerability (a weakness that attackers could exploit) in a popular app that millions of people use. You could: (A) Report it quietly to the company, (B) Sell information about it to the highest bidder, or (C) Say nothing and hope no one finds it.

Do this:

  1. Which option would you choose? Circle A, B, or C.
  2. Discuss with a partner or reflect in writing: What are the trade-offs of each option? Who benefits or is harmed by each?

  3. In one sentence, why did you choose your option?

Part 5 – Organizational culture (about 5 minutes)

Security culture means that everyone in an organization values and practices security—not because they fear punishment, but because it matters. A blame culture is when people hide mistakes because they are afraid of being blamed. In a blame culture, security incidents may go unreported, and lessons are not learned.

Do this:

  1. In one sentence, why might a blame culture make security worse?
  2. Design 2–3 actions a company could take to build a stronger security culture. (e.g. reward people for reporting suspicious emails, run regular training, make it easy to ask questions.)


Part 6 – Check what you learned (about 2 minutes)

Checklist. Put a ✓ in the box when it is true for you.

  • □ I know what the social side of cyber strategy involves (people, policy, culture, ethics).
  • □ I understand human factors in security (e.g. phishing, awareness).
  • □ I understand how responsibility is shared (individual, organization, government).
  • □ I have thought about ethical choices in cyber security.
  • □ I understand the difference between security culture and blame culture.
