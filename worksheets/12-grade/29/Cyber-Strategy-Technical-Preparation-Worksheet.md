## Part 2 – Threat modeling and risk (about 5 minutes)

**Threat modeling** means thinking about who might attack (e.g. hackers, insiders) and how (e.g. through email, weak passwords, unpatched software). An **attack surface** is all the points where an attacker could try to get in. **Risk** is the chance that a threat will happen and how bad the impact would be.

**Real-life scenario:** A small clinic stores patient records on one computer. The computer has no backup, the staff use simple passwords, and they open emails from unknown senders. One day, **ransomware** locks all files. The clinic cannot access patient data and must decide whether to pay the ransom.

## Part 3 – Defense-in-depth (about 5 minutes)

**Defense-in-depth** means using many layers of security. If one layer fails, others may still protect you. For example: strong passwords + two-factor authentication + firewalls + backups. No single layer is perfect.

**Discuss with a partner or reflect in writing:** Why might one layer fail? Why do organizations use multiple layers instead of just one?

## Part 4 – Incident response (about 5 minutes)

When a **security incident** happens (e.g. data breach, ransomware), organizations follow an **incident response** process: **Detect** → **Respond** → **Recover**. The order matters.

**Scenario:** A company discovers that customer data has been stolen. Some employees want to shut down everything immediately. Others want to investigate first.

## Part 5 – Security trade-offs (about 5 minutes)

Security decisions often involve **trade-offs**: more security can mean more cost or less convenience. For example, **stricter passwords** might reduce risk but frustrate users; **multi-factor authentication (MFA)** adds a step but blocks many attacks.

**Discuss with a partner or reflect in writing:** Would you rather use stricter passwords (longer, more complex) or add MFA (an extra step when you log in)? Why?

**Do this:**
