## Part 2 – Human factors in security (about 5 minutes)

**Human factors** in security mean how people’s behaviour affects safety. People make mistakes, trust too easily, or ignore warnings. **Phishing** is when attackers trick people into clicking links or giving passwords by pretending to be someone trustworthy. Training and awareness help, but they are not enough by themselves.

**Real-life scenario:** An employee at a company receives an email that looks like it is from the IT department. It says “Your password expires in 24 hours. Click here to update.” The employee clicks, enters their password, and within hours the company’s systems are breached. Customer data is stolen.

## Part 3 – Policy and responsibility (about 5 minutes)

Who is responsible when something goes wrong? **Individuals** (e.g. using strong passwords, not sharing credentials), **organizations** (e.g. training, policies, technical controls), and **governments** (e.g. laws, standards) all play a role. Responsibility is often shared.

**Scenario:** A student’s school account is hacked. The attacker posts embarrassing messages. The student used the same password on several sites, including one that was breached. The school did not require two-factor authentication.

## Part 5 – Organizational culture (about 5 minutes)

**Security culture** means that everyone in an organization values and practices security—not because they fear punishment, but because it matters. A **blame culture** is when people hide mistakes because they are afraid of being blamed. In a blame culture, security incidents may go unreported, and lessons are not learned.
